What we see

Kāraka is a fingerprint of each dictionary's purpose. Overall bhāve (action / abstract noun) dominates, but KRM inverts this — kartari 227 ≫ bhāve 30 — because the Kṛdanta-rūpa-mālā is built around agent-derivatives. The kāraka mix tells you what a dictionary is for.

The kāraka × pratyaya grid is textbook-clean Pāṇini. lyuṭ → bhāve + karaṇe (the -ana action / instrument nouns); kta spreads across bhāve / karmaṇi / kartari — exactly the past participle's three readings; ghañ → bhāve; lyu is pure kartari.

Affix polysemy is quantified by entropy. Generalists (ḍa, anīyar, ac ≈ 2 bits) form almost any kāraka; specialists (lyu 0.33, ṣṭran, aṅ) do one job — directly pedagogical.

Root productivity follows corpus frequency (kṛ ≫ bhū > i, dhā, gam), and the traditions agree: A cascade of root-recovery tiers — local citation, KRM's head-word-is-the-root, a cross-dict oracle (incl. KRM's 60k-form paradigm), and a DeepSeek pass over the residual, every fill validated against the canonical dhātu list — lifts the empty-root tail to near-complete (VCP 20 → 97 %, SHS → 95 %).

Kāraka × pratyaya (Sanskrit-side, pooled) ⤓ CSV

Each row is a primary affix (pratyaya); each column is a kāraka — the grammatical role the derived word plays in its root's action (the doer, the instrument, the object, or the bare action itself). A cell counts how many extracted derivations, pooled across the six Sanskrit-prose dictionaries, use that affix in that kāraka sense; darker means more. The row total on the right is the affix's overall frequency, so reading along a row shows how one affix divides its labour across senses, and reading down a column shows which affixes compete to express one relation. This grid exists only because the Sanskrit-side dictionaries spell the kāraka out in the entry (BAve GaY = "in the bhāva sense, affix ghañ"); WIL, MW and the German dictionaries never state it, so they contribute nothing here. Row and column labels link to their definition in the Dictionary of Sanskrit Grammar.

Read it: ① The lyuṭ × bhāve cell (1005) — ghañ-style action nouns in -ana are formed in the "the act of" sense ~1000× (e.g. karaṇa "doing"); it is lyuṭ's single commonest job. ② The kta row lights three columns at once (bhāve · karmaṇi · kartari) — the past participle is read as the action, the object, or the agent by context (kṛta = "the doing / the deed / the doer").
Can't tell you: this is a population view — it counts attestations of an affix-in-a-kāraka, not whether a particular word is unambiguous (a form like gata legitimately sits in two cells). And it cannot compare the English/German dicts, which are blank by construction, not by absence of derivation.

Affix kāraka-spread (entropy) ⤓ CSV

Each bar is the Shannon entropy, in bits, of one affix's distribution across the seven kārakas — a single number answering "how many different jobs does this affix do?" Zero bits means the affix only ever forms one kāraka (a pure specialist); the theoretical maximum (~2.8 bits) would mean it spreads perfectly evenly over all seven. High-entropy affixes are semantic generalists — the affix alone tells you almost nothing about the meaning, so you must read the gloss; low-entropy affixes are specialists that pin the sense by themselves. The entropy is computed only over the Sanskrit-side derivations that carry a kāraka, so the count it rests on (the n column in the CSV) matters as much as the bits. Affix labels link to their full grammar definition.

Read it: ① lyu at 0.33 bits is a pure specialist — see it and you know the word is an agent (kartari): kāraka "doer". ② ac at 1.84 bits over n=411 is a generalist — the bare affix predicts almost nothing; -a nouns can be agent, action, object or instrument, so you must consult the entry.
Can't tell you: entropy measures spread, not productivity or correctness — a rare affix with two even senses outscores a hugely productive affix that has one dominant sense plus a long tail. Always read it next to n and the heatmap; a high bar on a tiny n is noise.

Root productivity ⤓ CSV

Each bar counts how many distinct derived words trace back to that root across the verbal-root dictionaries (the Sanskrit-side dicts + MW + PWG/PW; WIL is excluded because its "root" is the first etymon, often a prefix). It is a measure of derivational fertility — which roots seed the most vocabulary in the recorded lexicon. The ranking tracks corpus frequency closely but is not the same thing: it counts dictionary head-words, not running corpus tokens. Click any root to open its full entry — paradigms, attested forms and primary derivatives — in Whitney's The Roots, Verb-Forms and Primary Derivatives of the Sanskrit Language (1885) on samskrtam.ru.

Read it: ① kṛ "do / make" tops the list — the most fertile root in the language, seeding kṛta, karaṇa, kartṛ, kārya, kriyā…; click it to see the whole Whitney paradigm. ② i "go" ranks near the top despite being a single short vowel — the oldest, shortest roots are disproportionately productive, a real signal about the age-vs-fertility relationship.
Can't tell you: the counts are inflated by coverage, not only real productivity — a root recorded in more of the ten dictionaries accrues more derivatives, and two homonymous roots (e.g. the two √vid "know" / "find") may be merged into one bar. It is a lexicon count, never a corpus frequency.

Cross-dictionary affix agreement ⤓ CSV

Take every head-word that two dictionaries both analyse with an affix, and ask: do they choose the same affix? The percentage is the share that agree; the bracket is the 95% Wilson confidence interval, which widens sharply when only a few head-words are shared. This is a direct test of whether two lexicographic traditions reconstruct the same Pāṇinian derivation for the same word — a consistency check that no single dictionary can give you. Rows are sorted by the number of shared head-words, so the most evidentially solid comparisons sit at the top.

Read it: ① Apte↔AP 100% [97.9–100] over 178 shared head-words — the two Apte editions are essentially identical, exactly the sanity check you'd want. ② WIL↔SKD 22.9% [14.6–34.0] — Wilson and the Śabdakalpadruma pick the same affix barely a fifth of the time, and the interval tops out at 34%, below every Sanskrit-side pair, so the gap is real, not small-sample noise.
Can't tell you: it scores only head-words that both dicts root with an affix — it is silent on coverage gaps (a word one dict omits never enters the comparison), and a tidy 100% over n=5 is weak evidence. Always read the CI and the shared-count together.

Cross-dictionary root agreement ⤓ CSV

The same comparison, but on the root instead of the affix — so it can include the dictionaries that attribute a root without naming a pratyaya: MW (via its √ notation and parse= field) and the German PWG/PW (via "Wurzel" / "von"). Root agreement runs lower than affix agreement, but that is a measurement artefact, not real disagreement: the same root is written in different transliterations and homonym conventions across traditions, so a "miss" is often two spellings of one dhātu, not a genuine dispute.

Read it: ① PWG↔PW ~93% — the two German Petersburg dictionaries, same school, agree on the root almost always. ② MW↔PWG ~65% — the English √-tradition and the German Wurzel-tradition independently land on the same root for two-thirds of shared head-words, a strong cross-tradition validation given the script differences.
Can't tell you: a low cell here may be a transliteration/homonym mismatch rather than a real conflict; treat root agreement as a lower bound on true agreement, and the affix table above as the cleaner signal.

Affix legend (Sanskrit → surface · function · Russian)

The Sanskrit affix names (ghañ, lyuṭ…) are opaque until you strip their mute markers (anubandhas). This table gives, for each affix shown above: its IAST surface suffix — what actually attaches to the stem once the markers fall away — its English function, and the Russian gloss from the Dictionary of Sanskrit Grammar. The affix name links to its full DSG entry. Use it as a key while reading the heatmap and entropy charts.

Read it: ① ghañ → -a, "action/result noun" — drop the gh and ñ markers and the affix is just -a; it makes a bhāva noun (pāka "cooking"). ② lyuṭ → -ana — the l and ṭ are mute; the real suffix is -ana (gamana "going").
Can't tell you: the surface is the affix in isolation — sandhi and root-gradation reshape the actual word (kṛ + lyuṭ → karaṇa, not kṛ-ana). The legend names the building block, not the finished form.

Root-capture coverage ⤓ CSV

Not every extracted derivation comes with its root sitting beside it; this table shows, per dictionary, how the root was recovered, tier by tier. local = the root sat next to the derivation marker; headword-root = the dictionary is organised by root so the head-word is the dhātu; nearest-root = the nearest known dhātu in a citation context; dhātupāṭha-join and oracle-join = filled by look-up against the canonical dhātu list and the cross-dictionary root oracle. The final column is the share of derivations that ended up with a root.

Read it: ① KRM is 100%, entirely via headword-root — it is a root dictionary, so every derivation's root is just the entry head-word. ② VCP reaches 97% as a stack: 2263 local + 532 nearest-root + 363 oracle-join + 361 DeepSeek llm-pass, leaving only 117 empty — read the row left-to-right to see each tier's contribution.
Can't tell you: the percentage is "has a root", not "has the correct root" — the nearest-root and oracle tiers are dhātu-list-validated but not hand-checked. Sample them with sample_nearest_root_audit.py before trusting a single fill.
